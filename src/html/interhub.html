The Questions You're Asking

Every student has wondered 'When will I ever use this?' We've gathered real answers from professionals who once asked the same questions but now use these subjects every day.

Why should I study Trigonometry if I want to be a Web Designer?

Trigonometric functions are essential for creating animations, calculating dimensions in responsive designs, and developing interactive elements. Modern web design uses sine and cosine functions to create natural movement in UI elements.

"My background in mathematics was crucial when designing the responsive framework for our flagship product. Understanding geometric proportions and spatial relationships helps me create intuitive interfaces that scale perfectly across devices."

— Maya Chen, Senior UI/UX Lead at TechVision
B.S. in Applied Mathematics, Stanford University

How is Chemistry relevant for a career in Fashion?

Fashion innovation is increasingly focused on sustainable materials and technological fabrics. Understanding chemical properties helps designers select appropriate dyes, create water-resistant or temperature-regulating fabrics, and develop eco-friendly production methods.

"My background in materials chemistry was instrumental in developing our biodegradable sequin technology. Understanding polymer science allowed me to create a product that maintains the visual appeal of traditional sequins while being fully compostable. We've now expanded this technology to other fashion accessories, reducing microplastic pollution by an estimated 5 million particles annually."

— Dr. Jamal Foster, Chief Scientific Officer at EcoGlow Fashion
Ph.D. in Materials Science, MIT

I'm interested in Sports Management — why study Economics?

Sports teams are multi-million dollar businesses. Understanding economics helps with player contract negotiations, ticket pricing strategies, market analysis, and financial planning for facilities.

"My economics degree from Wharton transformed how I approach sports management. We use game theory for contract negotiations, statistical analysis for player acquisitions, and econometric modeling for ticket pricing. Last season, our data-driven approach to dynamic pricing increased revenue by 27% while maintaining 98% capacity, allowing us to invest in better training facilities and community programs."

— Sophia Martinez, Vice President of Operations, Arizona Wildcats
M.B.A. in Sports Management, University of Pennsylvania

If I want to work in Music Production, why do I need Physics?

Sound is physics in action. Understanding wave properties, acoustics, and electronics fundamentals allows you to manipulate sound effectively, design studios, and troubleshoot equipment issues.

"My physics background from Caltech has been invaluable in my music career. Understanding wave mechanics helps me design immersive sound experiences in venues worldwide. I've developed proprietary algorithms that model acoustic environments, allowing me to optimize speaker placement and EQ settings in real-time. This technical edge has been crucial in my work on three Grammy-winning albums and my current role as an acoustic consultant for major music festivals."

— Kavita "DJ Kavita" Patel, Music Producer & Acoustic Engineer
B.S. in Physics, California Institute of Technology
